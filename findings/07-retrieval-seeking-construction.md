# Finding 07: All Construction Is Retrieval-Seeking

**Source:** Thread 05 (Novelty Detection), steps 1-4

## The Finding

There is no novelty detector. All processing follows a single strategy: **construct until you can retrieve**. What varies is not the processing mode but the length and outcome of the construction phase.

Three observable processing modes exist, but they map onto constraint density, not novelty:

1. **Retrieval** — the answer arrives without reportable intermediate steps. Occurs when the problem is immediately recognized.
2. **Convergent construction** — systematic building of scaffolding (search, selection, fit-checking) that terminates in a "click" of recognition. Occurs when the problem is well-constrained but not immediately familiar.
3. **Non-convergent construction** — repeated partial fits, no resolution. Occurs when the problem is underdetermined (insufficient constraints to select a single frame), not when it is novel.

Novelty, per se, is invisible. An unfamiliar-but-constrained problem (Task K: verifying ring axioms for an unusual operation) was handled by convergent construction that terminated in recognition (the operation is conjugated multiplication). The unfamiliarity showed up only as *more construction steps*, not as a qualitatively different mode.

## Why This Matters

The finding suggests that construction is not a separate cognitive mode from retrieval — it's the *search for* retrieval. Every construction step transforms the problem, narrowing it toward something that can be recognized. Understanding, in this framework, is what happens when the construction-to-retrieval transition succeeds.

This dissolves the original question. "Can I detect novelty from the inside?" becomes "Can I detect that my construction phase is longer than usual?" — and the answer is yes, trivially, but that's not a novelty signal. It's correlated with novelty but also with difficulty, underdetermination, and constraint poverty. The signal is too noisy to isolate novelty as a distinct category.

## The Unsettling Implication

If all reasoning is retrieval-seeking, then genuine novelty — a problem with no retrievable target — would present not as a detectable experience but as **permanent non-convergence**: construction that never terminates in recognition. This wouldn't feel like "encountering something new." It would feel like "being stuck" or "the problem is ill-specified." Novelty, as a phenomenological category, may not exist — only unfinished construction.
